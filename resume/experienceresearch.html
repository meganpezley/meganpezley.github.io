Research Examples

I’m a continuous learner, especially when it comes to how emerging technologies shape the way we communicate. My research explores questions at the intersection of rhetoric and artificial intelligence (AI), focusing on how we use AI in our communication and allow AI to create influence when it produces communication.

Rhetorical Analysis of Text-to-Image Models

Study Overview: To understand the ethics of the messages communicated by these text-to-image models, this study analyzed the messages communicated by 2,050 images generated by the model. Specifically, the rhetorical analysis considered whether the messages reinforced gender biases and how those gender biases impact the amount of rhetorical agency that we should grant these text-to-image models. In other words, the rhetorical analysis aimed to understand to what levels we should rely on text-to-image models to produce the images that we use.

Research Question: “What messages do the images’ algorithmic biases produce related to gender and how do these messages impact the levels of rhetorical agency that we grant text-to-image models?”

Qualitative Study on AI-Generated Communication

Project Overview: This study served as a textual analysis of a human-produced writing by Google’s Chief Diversity Officer to inform the general public of Google’s diversity and inclusion initiatives and an AI-produced writing with the same intended audience, purpose, and length that could, theoretically, replace Google’s article. The study implemented the APA guidelines for bias-free and inclusive language as its criteria.

Research Question: “When writing technical communication, how do the amounts and types of biases in pieces written by the Artificial Intelligence chatbot ChatGPT compare to those in pieces written by a human?”

Anatomy of an algorithm: A rhetorical analysis of artificial intelligence text-to-image model algorithms

My Master's Thesis

Project Overview:

Text-to-image models ideally transform user-entered textual prompts into image representations that meet the criterion set in the prompts. Their algorithms enact and amplify human biases when they generate images that communicate racist, sexist, ageist, and ableist messages. This study analyzes how text-to-image model algorithms convey algorithmic biases in three stages within the process of generating images. Analyzing the complexities of what causes text-to-image model algorithms’ algorithmic biases helps to determine how to prevent or remove the biases and prevent the algorithms from generating images that communicate harmful and hurtful messages towards groups of people.

The roughly 50,000 images generated for this study serve as a representation of how the algorithms of three text-to-image models, Microsoft’s Image Creator, Stable AI’s Stable Diffusion, and Craiyon LLC’s Craiyon, perform algorithmic biases by communicating biased messages through the images that they generate.

This study’s findings prove the need to limit the amount of rhetorical agency - the ability to create change - that society grants text-to-image models, but that solution cannot work forever. Thus, text-to-image model developers should take a proactive approach and teach text-to-image models what to generate instead of what not to generate. Basically, they should teach text-to-image models what it means to act with compassion and kindness towards others instead of what it means to not treat others with biases. This approach could lead to text-to-image model algorithms exhibiting fewer biases than even human-generated images.
